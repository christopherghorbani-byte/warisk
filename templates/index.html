How WarRisk Works
The Problem
Reconstruction companies won't enter a war zone without insurance. Banks won't lend. Investors won't commit. But standard insurance excludes war by default — and the specialized war-risk market is opaque, slow, and only accessible to companies with the right broker relationships. The result: billions in reconstruction sits frozen while buildings stay rubble.
1
The Bucket — and why it's not enough
Insurance is a shared risk pool. Businesses pay a premium into the bucket. When something bad happens, the bucket pays out. The insurer manages the bucket, prices the risk, and keeps a margin.

War risk breaks the model. One missile strike can drain the entire bucket at once. Insurers can't spread that across thousands of unrelated customers the way they do for car accidents. So they either refuse, charge prohibitively, or require so much paperwork that it takes months — while the war keeps going.
2
The fix: Parametric insurance
Traditional insurance pays out after someone proves their loss — which means sending an adjuster into an active war zone, waiting months, and arguing over damage estimates.

Parametric insurance skips all of that. Instead of "prove your loss," the policy says: "If satellite imagery confirms ≥25% structural damage to your asset, you get paid automatically within 10 business days. No adjuster. No argument."

The trigger is agreed upfront. When it fires, money moves. That's it.
3
Where WarRisk fits — the intelligence layer
WarRisk doesn't hold the bucket. That's still the insurer — Lloyd's syndicates, reinsurers, government-backed facilities. WarRisk is the brain that makes the bucket functional in places it was too scared to go before.

4
Why global — and why now
Conflict isn't one war anymore. Ukraine. Gaza. Sudan. The Black Sea. The Gulf of Hormuz. The Red Sea. Every one of these has reconstruction needs, shipping lanes, and infrastructure that somebody needs insured — and nobody has built a single platform that covers all of them.

The same risk intelligence engine works everywhere. The data sources are global. The parametric structure is the same. The insurers at Lloyd's already cover multiple conflict zones — they just need better tools to price and monitor them confidently. That's what WarRisk provides.
The business model — simply
WarRisk charges a platform fee — either a percentage of the premium it helps price, or a SaaS fee from the insurer or broker using the intelligence. We never hold risk capital. The bucket belongs to Lloyd's. The brain belongs to WarRisk.

Think of it like the difference between owning the cars and owning the dispatch system. Uber didn't need to buy a fleet. WarRisk doesn't need to be an insurance company.
